How AI Works · Chapter 2

Wait, what is a token?

We've been throwing the word "tokens" around. Time to stop. Before a model can read any text, that text gets chopped into small pieces called tokens, and every token becomes a number. Everything the model actually does, it does on numbers. Here's the round-trip for a simple legal question.

Model's answer
"Jurisprudence is the theory and philosophy of law."

A few things worth noticing:

  • "Jurisprudence" splits — twice, differently. In the input (lowercase, with a leading space) it becomes three tokens: ·juris, pr, udence. In the output (capital J, no leading space, sentence-start) it becomes four: Jur, is, pr, udence. Capitalization and position change the split.
  • Tokens reuse numeric IDs across contexts. pr is token 652 both times it appears. udence is 74193. ·is (with leading space) is 374. The vocabulary is fixed once during training — same word-pieces, same numbers, wherever they show up.
  • The model never actually sees English. Only numbers. Whatever it "knows" about grammar, meaning, or legal theory is computed from operations on these token IDs.
  • Callback to Chapter 1: the probability distribution on the bar chart? That's over all ~100,000 of these numbers. The model doesn't pick the next word — it picks the next number, and we decode it on the way back out.
How AI Works · Chapter 3

Meaning lives in space

Once text is tokens and tokens are numbers, the model's next move is to turn each number into a vector — a long list of numbers that plants the token somewhere in a high-dimensional space. The beautiful part: the model learns these positions so that words with similar meanings end up close together. Below, 42 real English words embedded by GloVe into 50 dimensions, projected down to 2 so you can actually see them.

42 words in vector space · PCA to 2D
Each dot is a word. Position shows where GloVe placed it in 50-dimensional meaning-space, squashed down to 2 via PCA for display. Hover any word to focus; hover a category label to filter.

A few things worth noticing:

  • Categories form visible clusters. Colors group together. Animals group together. Countries group together. Legal terms group together. Nothing in the training told GloVe these were "colors" or "animals" — it just read billions of words of text and learned that words appearing in similar contexts should have similar vectors. Meaning fell out of the math.
  • You're looking at a shadow. The real embedding is 50 directions at once. PCA projects that down to 2, which loses most of the information (the 2D plot keeps only ~34% of the original variance). Words that look close in this picture might be close in the real space, but not always. We'll add a "true nearest-neighbor" view next.
  • The axes don't mean anything. Unlike a normal chart, "up" and "right" aren't defined concepts. PCA just picks the two directions where the words spread out the most. A different word list would produce different axes.
  • This is what the "meaning" layer of the model is. Every operation downstream — attention, prediction, the whole transformer — runs on vectors like these. Understand this picture and you have the shape of everything the model does with meaning.
