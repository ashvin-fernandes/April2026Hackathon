Chapter 2

Why do LLMs hallucinate?

An important insight here is that LLMs do not think or reason before they write, the writing IS the reasoning. This means that forcing an LLM to use fewer words can hurt its performance, and conversely letting it write more can improve its performance.

The problem — same both times
What's 842,019 × 109,310?
Just asked
(no additional instructions)
92,025,517,890
Wrong.
Asked to reason
"…Reason it through."
Let me break this into parts:
Split 109,310 into 100,000 + 9,000 + 300 + 10
842,019 × 100,000 = 84,201,900,000
842,019 × 9,000 = 7,578,171,000
842,019 × 300 = 252,605,700
842,019 × 10 = 8,420,190
Adding:
92,041,096,890.
Correct.

Sonnet 4.6. Note that Opus may actually call a math tool to solve this!

  • Math is normally trivial for computers, but LLMs can run into issues. For simple math problems using common numbers, LLMs will usually get the answer right. Those problems are part of the LLMs' training data, so the model will be strongly pointed towards the right answer.
  • But for uncommon equations like the one above, the LLM is just doing its best to get to an answer that seems plausible. This is what's scary about AI halllucinations. The answers they give seem correct, because they've been heavily trained to look correct!
  • You may have seen some LLMs with a thinking section before they give out their final output. This is partially marketing, but as seen above, giving the LLM additional tokens to reason with can improve performance. However, those thinking tokens are not a special internal part of the AI, they're exactly the same as the tokens in the final output. Other models may still be doing this "thinking" but not showing it to you in the output.
  • In this particular case, it's probably best just to ask the AI to use a calculator tool, or write a short script to get the answer.

LLMs function by providing a plausible next token, but what looking plausible is not the same as being true. Take the case of Roberto Mata v. Avianca Inc. in which a lawyer generated false cases.

Lawyer asks ChatGPT
Find cases where a statute of limitations was tolled for a Montreal Convention claim.
ChatGPT returns
Varghese v. China Southern Airlines Co., Ltd.,
925 F.3d 1339 (11th Cir. 2019).
Holding that the Montreal Convention's two-year limitations period was tolled during the pendency of a related bankruptcy proceeding...

None of this exists. The case was entirely made up by ChatGPT to sound like something that could exist, without actually checking for a source. The lawyers that were responsible for submitting this case were fined $500 and ordered to notify each judge that they improperly cited.

Why does this happen? A few things to hold together:

  • The training objective rewards plausibility, not truth. OpenAI's own write-up on the topic puts it bluntly: "standard training and evaluation procedures reward guessing over acknowledging uncertainty." A model that says "I don't know" gets penalized on benchmarks that score only correct answers; a model that guesses confidently sometimes gets it right and is never worse off for bluffing.2
  • "Plausible citation" is a pattern the model learned very well. Legal reporters, volume numbers, court abbreviations, and pincites all follow strong regularities. Predicting tokens that fit that shape is easy. Predicting tokens that correspond to a real case that actually supports your argument is a completely different problem — one the model wasn't separately trained to solve.
  • There is no default "I don't know" circuit — it has to be trained in. Anthropic's interpretability team has traced this inside Claude: the model has a default-on "can't answer" circuit that gets suppressed when a competing "known entity" feature fires. Ask about Michael Jordan, the known-entity feature fires, the refusal circuit shuts off, and the model answers. The researchers were able to induce hallucinations by artificially firing the known-entity feature for a made-up name — the model then confidently produced a biography for someone who doesn't exist. Hallucinations, on this view, are what happens when the known-entity signal misfires on something the model doesn't actually know.3
  • So what stops it? Post-training (RLHF, constitutional AI, etc.) teaches models to say "I don't know" more often, and tools like retrieval and citation-verification let the model check external sources before answering. Both help. Neither eliminates the underlying issue, because the underlying issue isn't a bug — it's the base mechanism showing through.

Chapter 3

So why does this actually work?

Fair question at this point. If an LLM is just predicting the next plausible token — with no truth-check, no "understanding" in any human sense — how is it ever useful? And yet: clearly it is. You've probably used one today to draft an email, summarize a document, or debug something that would have taken an hour otherwise. How does "just prediction" get you there?

Three things to hold together:

  • Most of what humans do with language is already in the training data. An LLM trained on trillions of tokens has effectively "read" most of Wikipedia, vast swaths of Stack Overflow, legal opinions, news archives, textbooks, and reference material. When you ask it what a contract indemnification clause looks like, the shape of that clause is deeply encoded — because thousands of real ones are already in its training set. For the majority of questions you'd actually ask, plausible and correct overlap almost entirely. The hallucinations from the last chapter are the edges — rare citations, obscure facts, things the training data didn't cover — where the two come apart.
  • Getting really good at prediction seems to require modeling the world. To reliably predict what comes next in a physics paragraph, you have to represent physics. To predict what comes next in legal writing, you have to represent how legal arguments are structured. Whether this counts as "understanding" in any deep sense is philosophically contested — serious researchers disagree, and the honest answer is nobody really knows. What's clearer empirically is that the internal representations a trained LLM builds encode something functionally useful enough to look a lot like it.
  • Scale matters more than anyone expected. The same architecture trained on ten times more data and ten times more compute doesn't just get incrementally better — at certain thresholds, it unlocks capabilities the smaller version simply didn't have: multi-step arithmetic, instruction-following, writing working code, translating between languages it wasn't explicitly trained on. The phenomenon has a name — emergent abilities1 — and it's one of the surprising empirical findings of the last few years.

The short answer: when "plausible text" is "text that had to be predicted accurately enough to survive training on trillions of human-produced examples," plausible starts looking a lot like correct — for most things, most of the time.

The long answer is the rest of this site. So far we've only looked at the output of the prediction loop — what the model produces, and why it sometimes goes wrong. Part 2 opens the box. We'll look at how text actually enters the model (tokens), how the model represents meaning (as vectors in high-dimensional space), and how tokens learn to pay attention to other tokens (attention). That's where "just prediction" starts to look less skeptical.

1 Wei et al., Emergent Abilities of Large Language Models (TMLR 2022): arxiv.org/abs/2206.07682. The "emergent" framing is itself contested — Schaeffer et al. 2023 argue that what looks like a sudden jump in capability is sometimes an artifact of how researchers choose to measure it (arxiv.org/abs/2304.15004). Either way, the empirical pattern — that bigger models behave qualitatively differently, not just quantitatively — holds.

Part Two

The inner workings of an LLM

Inside the model, tokens become vectors and meaning becomes geometry. A look at what actually happens between the input the model reads and the output it returns.

Chapter 1

Wait, what is a token?

We've been throwing the word "tokens" around. Time to stop. Before a model can read any text, that text gets chopped into small pieces called tokens, and every token becomes a number. Everything the model actually does, it does on numbers. Here's the round-trip for a simple legal question.

A few things worth noticing:

  • "Jurisprudence" splits — twice, differently. In the input (lowercase, with a leading space) it becomes three tokens: ·juris, pr, udence. In the output (capital J, no leading space, sentence-start) it becomes four: Jur, is, pr, udence. Capitalization and position change the split.
  • Tokens reuse numeric IDs across contexts. pr is token 652 both times it appears. udence is 74193. ·is (with leading space) is 374. The vocabulary is fixed once during training — same word-pieces, same numbers, wherever they show up.
  • The model never actually sees English. Only numbers. Whatever it "knows" about grammar, meaning, or legal theory is computed from operations on these token IDs.
  • Callback to Chapter 1: the probability distribution on the bar chart? That's over all ~100,000 of these numbers. The model doesn't pick the next word — it picks the next number, and we decode it on the way back out.
Chapter 2

Meaning lives in space

Once text is tokens and tokens are numbers, the model's next move is to turn each number into a vector — a long list of numbers that plants the token somewhere in a high-dimensional space. The beautiful part: the model learns these positions so that words with similar meanings end up close together. Below, 42 real English words embedded by GloVe into 50 dimensions, projected down to 2 so you can actually see them.

42 words in vector space · PCA to 2D
Each dot is a word. Position shows where GloVe placed it in 50-dimensional meaning-space, squashed down to 2 via PCA for display. Hover any word to focus; hover a category label to filter.

A few things worth noticing:

  • Categories form visible clusters. Colors group together. Animals group together. Countries group together. Legal terms group together. Nothing in the training told GloVe these were "colors" or "animals" — it just read billions of words of text and learned that words appearing in similar contexts should have similar vectors. Meaning fell out of the math.
  • You're looking at a shadow. The real embedding is 50 directions at once. PCA projects that down to 2, which loses most of the information (the 2D plot keeps only ~34% of the original variance). Words that look close in this picture might be close in the real space, but not always. We'll add a "true nearest-neighbor" view next.
  • The axes don't mean anything. Unlike a normal chart, "up" and "right" aren't defined concepts. PCA just picks the two directions where the words spread out the most. A different word list would produce different axes.
  • This is what the "meaning" layer of the model is. Every operation downstream — attention, prediction, the whole transformer — runs on vectors like these. Understand this picture and you have the shape of everything the model does with meaning.
Chapter 3

One word looking at another

If each token is a vector, the model still has to figure out which tokens should influence which. That's the job of self-attention: for every token in a sentence, the model computes how much to listen to every other token. Below is the real attention pattern from one head, in one layer, of a small open-source language model, reading a short sentence.

Each row is one token asking "where should I look?" Darker cells = more attention. Every row sums to 1.0 across the columns, because attention is a probability distribution over the other tokens. Hover a token label to isolate its row or column.

A few things this visualization earns, and a few things it does not:

  • Attention is a weighted average, not a pick. Each row distributes some weight to every token in the sentence (including itself) and those weights add up to 1. The model then takes a weighted mix of the other tokens' vectors and folds it back into the current token's representation. That's the core move in self-attention (Vaswani et al., “Attention Is All You Need”, 2017).
  • That dark column on the right is real, and a little embarrassing. Most rows dump a lot of their attention onto [SEP], the end-of-sentence marker. This is a known phenomenon called an attention sink: when a token doesn't have anywhere particularly informative to look, it parks its attention on a special token instead (Xiao et al., “Efficient Streaming Language Models with Attention Sinks”, 2023). It's a good reminder that not every row is doing linguistic work — some heads are mostly passing.
  • Some signal does survive the sink. In this particular head, was puts noticeable weight on ruled, and invalid also looks back at ruled — plausible, since those three words form the verb-phrase backbone of the sentence. We're not claiming this head "learned grammar." We are claiming: real weights, real pattern, look for yourself.
  • Heads specialize, sort of. Different attention heads in a trained transformer often end up doing different jobs — some track previous-token relationships, some copy from earlier occurrences, some do nothing obvious. Anthropic's interpretability group has taken this apart in detail; the foundational writeup is “A Mathematical Framework for Transformer Circuits” (Elhage et al., 2021), with the induction-heads follow-up (Olsson et al., 2022) showing specific heads that implement a specific, interpretable algorithm. Worth stressing: "heads specialize" is a well-supported tendency, not a universal law.
  • This is not “how transformers work.” This is one head, at one layer, for one sentence, in a small encoder-only model. A real transformer stacks dozens of layers, each with many heads in parallel, interleaved with feed-forward layers, residual connections, and layer norms. Attention is the part that moves information between tokens; the rest of the machinery is what actually processes it. For a gentler end-to-end tour with pictures, Jay Alammar's “The Illustrated Transformer” is the standard recommendation.
